Time to Therapeutic Effect

IMMEDIATE (minutes–hours)

Benzodiazepines (Valium, Xanax, Ativan) · Propranolol (take 1–2 hrs before event)

2–4 WEEKS

SSRIs — Fluoxetine, Sertraline, Paroxetine, Escitalopram · SNRIs — Venlafaxine, Duloxetine · TCAs · Bupropion

2–6 WEEKS

Buspirone — Patient MUST know this. Not for acute anxiety. Takes time but has NO dependence risk.

Fluoxetine Special Rule

Half-life is 2–6 days. When switching FROM fluoxetine TO a MAOI, wait 5 WEEKS (not just 14 days).

SSRIs

Selective Serotonin Reuptake Inhibitors · First-line antidepressants

SSRI
Fluoxetine · Sertraline · Paroxetine
Prozac · Zoloft · Paxil
+
Mechanism
  • Block serotonin reuptake
  • ↑ serotonin in synapse
  • Does NOT affect NE/dopamine
Indications
  • Major depressive disorder
  • Anxiety disorders
  • OCD (fluoxetine especially)
  • PTSD · Panic disorder
Common Side Effects
  • Sexual dysfunction ★ MOST COMMON
  • Nausea (give with food)
  • Insomnia · Headache
  • Weight gain long-term
Expected Response
  • Improved mood in 2–4 weeks
  • Decreased anxiety
  • Better concentration
  • Improved sleep patterns
🚨
Life-Threatening
  • Serotonin Syndrome — if combined with MAOIs, SNRIs, St. John's Wort
  • Suicidal ideation — especially in patients under 25 in first 1–2 weeks
⭐
Top Nursing Priorities
  • Takes 2–4 weeks — educate patient NOT to stop early
  • Monitor suicidal ideation especially in youth <25
  • NEVER combine with MAOIs — 14-day washout required
  • Watch for serotonin syndrome: agitation, fever, diaphoresis, clonus
  • Taper slowly — do not stop abruptly
SSRI — QT RISK
Escitalopram · Citalopram
Lexapro · Celexa
+
Same as other SSRIs plus…
  • Escitalopram = more selective (fewer side effects)
  • Citalopram = QT prolongation risk ⚠
Indications
  • Depression · Panic disorder
  • PTSD · Social anxiety
🫀
Citalopram: QT Prolongation — Critical!
  • Can cause Torsades de Pointes → ventricular fibrillation → sudden death
  • Monitor ECG — especially in elderly
  • Do NOT combine with other QT-prolonging drugs

🧠 SSRI Mnemonic: "FINS"

F
Fluoxetine (Prozac) — longest half-life; 5-week washout before MAOI
I
Insomnia + sexual dysfunction — most common side effects to teach
N
Never with MAOIs — serotonin syndrome risk; 2-week washout minimum
S
Suicide watch — especially first weeks and patients under age 25
💊

SNRIs

Serotonin-Norepinephrine Reuptake Inhibitors · Dual action · First-line

SNRI
Venlafaxine · Duloxetine
Effexor · Cymbalta
+
Mechanism
  • Block reuptake of serotonin AND norepinephrine
  • Dual action = broader effects
  • More norepinephrine → raises BP
Indications
  • Major depressive disorder
  • GAD (generalized anxiety)
  • Neuropathic pain ★ duloxetine
  • Fibromyalgia (duloxetine)
Side Effects
  • Nausea — MOST COMMON
  • Headache · Insomnia
  • Sweating · Nervousness
  • HYPERTENSION ★ (NE effect)
Expected Response
  • Improved mood in 2–4 weeks
  • Reduced anxiety & pain
  • Better sleep & focus
🚨
Life-Threatening
  • Hypertension — NE stimulation can dangerously raise BP
  • Serotonin Syndrome if combined with MAOIs
⭐
Critical Nursing Actions
  • MONITOR BLOOD PRESSURE — #1 SNRI nursing action
  • DO NOT stop abruptly — discontinuation syndrome (electric shocks, dizziness)
  • 14-day MAOI washout required
  • Duloxetine: monitor liver function; avoid in liver disease
💡
SSRI vs SNRI — Key Exam Difference
  • SSRIs → serotonin only → no BP concern
  • SNRIs → serotonin + norepinephrine → MUST monitor blood pressure
  • Duloxetine treats PAIN (fibromyalgia, diabetic neuropathy) — SSRIs do NOT
💊

TCAs — Tricyclic Antidepressants

Older class · Second-line · HIGH overdose lethality

TCA
Imipramine · Amitriptyline · Doxepin
Tofranil · Elavil · Sinequan
+
Mechanism
  • Block reuptake of NE + serotonin
  • Also block histamine, ACh, alpha-1 receptors
  • Anticholinergic effects = MANY side effects
Indications
  • Depression (2nd line)
  • Neuropathic pain
  • Insomnia (doxepin)
  • Bedwetting/enuresis (imipramine)
Anticholinergic Side Effects
  • Sedation — give at BEDTIME
  • Dry mouth · Constipation
  • Urinary retention
  • Orthostatic hypotension
  • Blurred vision
Expected Response
  • Improved mood
  • Better sleep (often first)
  • Decreased neuropathic pain
💀
CRITICAL — Overdose = FATAL
  • CARDIOTOXICITY — fatal dysrhythmias (wide QRS, QT prolongation)
  • #1 antidepressant class associated with death by overdose
  • Monitor ECG regularly
⭐
Priority Nursing Actions
  • Provide SMALL prescription supply only — suicide risk
  • Take at NIGHT — use sedation therapeutically
  • FALL PRECAUTIONS — orthostatic hypotension
  • NEVER give with Benadryl — additive anticholinergic toxicity
  • Avoid alcohol — CNS depression + OD risk

🧠 TCA Side Effects: "ANTICHO"

A
Arrhythmias — most dangerous; monitor ECG always
N
Night dosing — give at bedtime due to sedation
T
Tiny supply only — limit script; lethal overdose risk
I
Inhibited bladder — urinary retention; assess output
C
Constipation + dry mouth — classic anticholinergic effects
H
Hypotension (orthostatic) — fall precautions; rise slowly
O
OD risk is LETHAL — narrowest therapeutic window of any antidepressant
⚠️

MAOIs

Monoamine Oxidase Inhibitors · LAST LINE · Strictest dietary & drug restrictions

MAOI — LAST LINE
Phenelzine · Isocarboxazid · Selegiline
Nardil · Marplan · Emsam patch
+
Mechanism
  • Inhibit MAO enzyme → can't break down monoamines
  • ↑ serotonin, NE, dopamine simultaneously
  • Effect is IRREVERSIBLE (weeks to recover)
Indications
  • Depression — LAST LINE ONLY
  • Atypical depression
  • Failed all other antidepressants
Side Effects
  • Orthostatic hypotension
  • Weight gain · Insomnia
  • Dry mouth · Sexual dysfunction
Foods to AVOID (Tyramine)
  • Aged cheeses (cheddar, blue)
  • Cured meats (salami, pepperoni)
  • Red wine · Tap/draft beer
  • Soy sauce · Miso · Tofu
  • Overripe / fermented foods
🚨
Hypertensive Crisis — #1 MAOI Emergency
  • Tyramine accumulates → massive NE release → BP skyrockets
  • Symptoms: severe headache, hypertension, palpitations, diaphoresis, stroke
  • SSRIs + MAOIs = serotonin syndrome (FATAL)
  • Meperidine (Demerol) + MAOIs = FATAL
  • Decongestants + MAOIs = hypertensive crisis
⭐
Critical Nursing Actions
  • Educate strict low-tyramine diet BEFORE first dose
  • Monitor BP closely — orthostatic and hypertensive crisis
  • 14-day washout before starting SSRIs
  • Avoid ALL OTC decongestants (pseudoephedrine)
  • Avoid St. John's Wort

🧠 Tyramine Foods: "CALM VASE"

C
Cheese (aged) — cheddar, parmesan, blue — highest risk
A
Alcohol — especially red wine and tap beer
L
Legumes — fava beans particularly
M
Meats (cured) — salami, pepperoni, hot dogs, smoked fish
V
Vermented foods — soy sauce, miso, sauerkraut
A
Avocado — large amounts
S
Smoked fish — smoked salmon, herring
E
Everything overripe — aging = tyramine accumulation

Bupropion

Wellbutrin / Zyban · Dopamine + NE reuptake inhibitor · Atypical antidepressant

ATYPICAL ANTIDEPRESSANT
Bupropion
Wellbutrin (depression) · Zyban (smoking cessation)
+
Mechanism
  • ↑ Dopamine + norepinephrine (NOT serotonin)
  • Unique vs SSRIs and SNRIs
  • Dopamine boost → mood + motivation
Indications
  • Major depression
  • Smoking cessation (Zyban)
  • ADHD (off-label)
  • Seasonal affective disorder
Side Effects
  • Insomnia → give in MORNING
  • Dry mouth · Headache
  • Tachycardia
  • Weight LOSS (not gain)
  • SEIZURES ⚠
What Makes it DIFFERENT
  • ✅ NO sexual dysfunction
  • ✅ NO weight gain
  • ✅ Smoking cessation use
  • ❌ SEIZURE risk = unique danger
⚡
Absolute Contraindications
  • SEIZURE DISORDER — lowers seizure threshold
  • Eating disorders (bulimia/anorexia) — higher seizure risk
  • Head trauma / brain injury
  • Abrupt alcohol or benzo withdrawal
✅
Key Patient Teaching
  • Take in the MORNING — causes insomnia if at night
  • Does NOT cause sexual dysfunction — unique SSRI advantage
  • Avoid MAOIs (14-day washout)
  • For smoking: reduces nicotine cravings; start before quit date

St. John's Wort

Herbal supplement · Often unreported · HIGH interaction risk

HERBAL ANTIDEPRESSANT
St. John's Wort
Hypericum perforatum — OTC herbal supplement
+
Mechanism
  • ↑ Serotonin, NE, dopamine
  • Induces CYP450 enzymes → reduces other drug levels
Use
  • Mild to moderate depression
  • OTC — often self-prescribed
  • Patients often don't report it
Side Effects
  • Photosensitivity ☀️ — use sunscreen
  • GI upset · Fatigue
  • Dry mouth · Dizziness
Drugs It Interferes With
  • Oral contraceptives → pregnancy risk
  • Warfarin → clot risk
  • HIV medications → treatment failure
  • Cyclosporine → organ rejection
🚨
Serotonin Syndrome Risk
  • NEVER combine with SSRIs, SNRIs, MAOIs, TCAs
  • Can cause life-threatening serotonin syndrome
🩺
Always Ask About Herbals
  • Patients don't think herbals = medications — ALWAYS ask
  • Educate: photosensitivity, birth control failure, warfarin interaction
😴

Benzodiazepines

CNS depressants · Acute anxiety · High dependence risk

BENZODIAZEPINE
Diazepam · Alprazolam · Lorazepam
Valium · Xanax · Ativan
+
Mechanism
  • Enhance GABA activity (inhibitory NT)
  • Bind GABA-A receptor → ↑ chloride influx → CNS depression
  • Anxiolytic + sedative + muscle relaxant + anticonvulsant
Indications
  • Acute anxiety / panic attacks
  • Insomnia · Seizures
  • Alcohol withdrawal (diazepam)
  • Status epilepticus (lorazepam)
Side Effects
  • Sedation / drowsiness
  • Confusion (especially elderly)
  • Dizziness · Ataxia
  • ↓ Respiratory rate
  • Anterograde amnesia
Expected Response
  • Rapid anxiety relief (minutes)
  • Calmness · Improved sleep
  • Muscle relaxation
🫁
Life-Threatening
  • Respiratory depression (esp. + opioids or alcohol) → FATAL
  • Physical dependence develops within weeks
  • Withdrawal SEIZURES — FATAL if stopped abruptly after long-term use
  • Reversal agent: FLUMAZENIL (short-acting — resedation can occur)
⭐
Priority Nursing Actions
  • Monitor respiratory rate — must be ≥12/min
  • NEVER combine with alcohol or opioids
  • TAPER dose — never abrupt discontinuation
  • FALL PRECAUTIONS — sedation + dizziness
  • Short-term use only for anxiety management
💡
Benzo vs Buspirone — Know the Difference
  • BENZO: Acute anxiety, immediate relief — but dependence risk is HIGH
  • BUSPIRONE: Chronic GAD, no dependence — BUT takes 2–6 weeks to work
  • Cannot swap overnight — must taper benzo first before switching
🌀

Buspirone

Atypical anxiolytic · No addiction potential · Preferred for GAD long-term

ATYPICAL ANXIOLYTIC
Buspirone
Buspar · Partial serotonin (5-HT1A) agonist
+
Mechanism
  • Partial agonist at 5-HT1A serotonin receptors
  • Also affects dopamine receptors
  • Does NOT affect GABA (unlike benzos)
Indications
  • Generalized Anxiety Disorder (GAD)
  • Long-term anxiety management
  • NOT for acute anxiety or panic
Side Effects
  • Dizziness · Headache
  • Nausea · Nervousness
  • Lightheadedness
Why Buspirone is Special
  • ✅ NO abuse/addiction potential
  • ✅ No physical dependence
  • ✅ No sedation or respiratory depression
  • ❌ Takes 2–6 WEEKS — patient must know!
📚
Key Patient Teaching
  • Takes 2–6 weeks — will NOT work right away
  • NOT for panic attacks or immediate anxiety relief
  • AVOID grapefruit juice — inhibits CYP3A4 → drug toxicity
  • No habit-forming — this is a major advantage over benzos
❤️

Beta Blockers — Propranolol

Performance anxiety · Somatic symptom relief

BETA BLOCKER
Propranolol
Inderal — non-selective beta-adrenergic blocker
+
Mechanism
  • Blocks beta-1 + beta-2 receptors
  • Blocks sympathetic (fight-or-flight) response
  • Reduces HR, BP, tremors, sweating
For Anxiety
  • Performance anxiety (public speaking)
  • Reduces PHYSICAL symptoms only
  • Does NOT reduce emotional/cognitive anxiety
Side Effects
  • Bradycardia · Hypotension
  • Fatigue · Dizziness
  • Bronchospasm in asthma
  • Cold extremities
Expected Response
  • Reduced tremors
  • Slower, calmer heart rate
  • Reduced sweating
  • Less physical anxiety symptoms
💓
Critical Assessments
  • HOLD if HR <60 bpm — report to provider
  • Severe bradycardia / heart block are life-threatening
  • Use CAUTIOUSLY in asthma (beta-2 blockade → bronchospasm)
  • Do NOT stop abruptly → rebound hypertension / angina
🕐
Timing
  • Take 1–2 hours BEFORE the anxiety-provoking event
  • Not for daily chronic anxiety management
🚨

Danger Zones

Serotonin syndrome, hypertensive crisis, respiratory depression — know all three cold.

🧪

Serotonin Syndrome

🚨
What Causes It?
  • Too much serotonin in the CNS — usually from combining two serotonergic drugs
  • SSRI + MAOI = classic · SSRI + St. John's Wort · SSRI + Tramadol · MAOI + any serotonergic drug
🧠

Mental Status

  • Agitation
  • Confusion
  • Anxiety
  • Restlessness
🫀

Autonomic

  • Fever (hyperthermia)
  • Diaphoresis
  • Tachycardia
  • Hypertension
  • Diarrhea
💪

Neuromuscular

  • Clonus ★ key sign
  • Hyperreflexia
  • Tremor · Ataxia
  • Myoclonus

NCLEX Practice Questions

15 questions — SATA, priority, appropriate response, unexpected outcome. Full rationales included.

Question 1Select All That Apply
A nurse is providing discharge teaching to a client prescribed sertraline (Zoloft). Which statements should be included? Select all that apply.
  • A "This medication may take 2–4 weeks before you notice mood improvement."
  • B "You can stop taking this once you feel better."
  • C "Avoid taking St. John's Wort while on this medication."
  • D "Sexual dysfunction is a common side effect."
  • E "Do not take this medication if you are also on a MAOI."
  • F "Take only on an empty stomach."
✓ Correct: A, C, D, E
Rationale

A – Correct. SSRIs have a delayed onset of 2–4 weeks. The patient must understand this to avoid stopping early.

B – Incorrect. Never stop abruptly — discontinuation syndrome (flu-like symptoms, electric shocks, dizziness). Must taper slowly.

C – Correct. St. John's Wort + SSRI = serotonin syndrome risk — life-threatening.

D – Correct. Sexual dysfunction (decreased libido, anorgasmia) is the most common reason patients stop SSRIs. Teaching prevents surprise and non-adherence.

E – Correct. SSRIs + MAOIs = fatal serotonin syndrome. 14-day washout required.

F – Incorrect. SSRIs can be taken with or without food. Taking with food actually reduces GI side effects.

Question 2Unexpected Outcome
A client taking venlafaxine (Effexor) returns for follow-up. Which finding is most concerning and unexpected?
  • A Reports mild nausea after the morning dose.
  • B Blood pressure has increased from 118/72 to 152/96 mmHg.
  • C Reports improved mood and better concentration.
  • D Reports mild difficulty sleeping.
✓ Correct: B
Rationale

B – Concerning. SNRIs block norepinephrine reuptake → stimulates alpha-1 receptors → raises blood pressure. A significant BP increase (118/72 → 152/96) is the #1 SNRI monitoring priority and requires immediate provider notification.

A – Expected. Nausea is the most common SNRI side effect; usually resolves. Take with food.

C – Desired outcome. Improved mood is the therapeutic goal.

D – Mildly expected. Insomnia can occur with SNRIs due to norepinephrine stimulation. Worth monitoring, but not as urgent as dangerous BP elevation.

Question 3Priority
A client has just been prescribed phenelzine (Nardil). Which nursing action is the highest priority?
  • A Teach about the delayed onset of 2–4 weeks.
  • B Educate the client about a strict low-tyramine diet.
  • C Instruct to take the medication with food.
  • D Advise to wear sunscreen when outdoors.
✓ Correct: B
Rationale

B – Priority (Safety first). The low-tyramine diet is a SAFETY issue. Failure to follow it can cause a hypertensive crisis — severe headache, sky-high BP, stroke, death. This is life-threatening and must be taught before the first dose.

A – Important but not #1. Delayed onset is important for adherence but not immediately life-threatening.

C – Not applicable. No special food timing restriction applies to MAOIs; tyramine-rich foods are the concern, not timing.

D – Wrong drug. Photosensitivity = St. John's Wort, not MAOIs.

Question 4Select All That Apply
A nurse is assessing a client taking lorazepam (Ativan). Which findings require immediate provider notification? Select all that apply.
  • A Respiratory rate of 9 breaths/min
  • B Client reports feeling calm and less anxious
  • C Client reports drinking two glasses of wine last night
  • D Slurred speech and ataxia noted
  • E Mild drowsiness
  • F Client stopped the medication abruptly 2 days ago
✓ Correct: A, C, D, F
Rationale

A – Report immediately. RR of 9 is critically low (normal minimum = 12). Respiratory depression = medical emergency. Prepare flumazenil (reversal agent).

B – Expected/desired. Calmness is the therapeutic goal — no reporting needed.

C – Report urgently. Alcohol + benzodiazepine = additive CNS depression = FATAL combination. Critical safety teaching moment.

D – Report. Slurred speech and ataxia indicate toxicity / over-sedation beyond expected mild drowsiness.

E – Expected. Mild drowsiness is a known, expected side effect. Monitor but not reportable alone.

F – Report immediately. Abrupt benzo discontinuation after months of use = life-threatening withdrawal seizures.

Question 5Appropriate Response
A client on bupropion (Wellbutrin) for 3 days calls saying it's "not working" and wants to stop. Which nurse response is most appropriate?
  • A "You're right — 3 days is enough to know if it works."
  • B "This is completely normal. Antidepressants take 2–4 weeks for full effect. Please continue as directed."
  • C "Stop it now and I'll switch you to a benzodiazepine that works faster."
  • D "Double your dose to reach therapeutic levels faster."
✓ Correct: B
Rationale

B – Correct. Antidepressants (including bupropion) require 2–4 weeks for full therapeutic effect. Education promotes adherence.

A – Incorrect and inaccurate. 3 days is far too early to assess efficacy.

C – Incorrect. Benzodiazepines are not antidepressants and should not replace bupropion for depression.

D – Dangerous. Doubling bupropion dose significantly raises seizure risk — the drug's most dangerous adverse effect. Dose changes require a provider order.

Question 6Select All That Apply
A client is prescribed amitriptyline (Elavil). Which nursing instructions are appropriate? Select all that apply.
  • A "Take this medication at bedtime."
  • B "You may take Benadryl if you have an allergic reaction."
  • C "Change positions slowly when getting out of bed."
  • D "Avoid drinking alcohol while on this medication."
  • F "Report unusual heart palpitations to your provider."
✓ Correct: A, C, D, F
Rationale

A – Correct. TCAs are highly sedating — bedtime administration uses this therapeutically and reduces daytime impairment.

B – INCORRECT — DANGEROUS. Benadryl (diphenhydramine) + TCA = additive anticholinergic toxicity: extreme dry mouth, urinary retention, confusion, tachycardia, constipation.

C – Correct. TCAs cause orthostatic hypotension. Slow position changes prevent falls and syncope.

D – Correct. Alcohol enhances CNS depression and dramatically increases overdose risk.

F – Correct. TCAs cause QT prolongation and arrhythmias. Palpitations must be reported immediately for ECG evaluation.

Question 7Unexpected Outcome
A client started buspirone (Buspar) 3 days ago. The client calls saying they still feel anxious. What is the most accurate nurse response?
  • A "Stop taking it and call the provider for a different prescription."
  • B "This is expected — buspirone takes 2–6 weeks to reach full effect. Continue as directed."
  • C "Take an extra dose when anxious for faster relief."
  • D "Add grapefruit juice to help the medication absorb faster."
✓ Correct: B
Rationale

B – Correct. Buspirone requires 2–6 weeks of consistent use. Day 3 is completely expected — the medication needs time to modulate serotonin receptors.

A – Premature. Stopping after 3 days means the drug never had a chance to work. Educate and encourage continued use.

C – Incorrect. Extra doses don't speed onset and increase side effects without any benefit.

D – DANGEROUS. Grapefruit INHIBITS CYP3A4 → buspirone accumulates → toxicity. Patients must AVOID grapefruit juice.

Question 8Priority
A client who takes fluoxetine (Prozac) has just been prescribed tramadol by an on-call provider for pain. What is the nurse's priority action?
  • A Administer the tramadol as prescribed and monitor pain levels.
  • B Hold the tramadol and contact the provider regarding serotonin syndrome risk.
  • C Administer half the dose to reduce risk.
  • D Document the order and notify the charge nurse only.
✓ Correct: B
Rationale

B – Priority. Tramadol has serotonergic properties (weakly inhibits serotonin reuptake). Combining with an SSRI significantly raises serotonin syndrome risk. HOLD the medication and contact the provider — this is patient advocacy and safety.

A – Incorrect. Administering without flagging a known dangerous interaction violates the nurse's safety duty.

C – Incorrect. Halving the dose doesn't eliminate the interaction risk, and the nurse cannot independently adjust doses.

D – Insufficient. Documenting without direct provider notification is negligent when patient harm is possible.

Question 9Select All That Apply
A client taking phenelzine (Nardil) asks which foods to avoid. Select all to include in teaching.
  • A Aged cheddar cheese
  • B Fresh grilled chicken breast
  • C Red wine
  • D Salami and pepperoni
  • E Fresh steamed broccoli
  • F Soy sauce and miso soup
  • G Tap/draft beer
✓ Correct: A, C, D, F, G
Rationale

MAOIs prevent MAO from breaking down tyramine → tyramine accumulates → massive NE release → hypertensive crisis.

A – AVOID: Aged cheddar is extremely high in tyramine — highest risk food.

B – SAFE: Fresh chicken is fine. The problem is aged, fermented, or cured foods — not fresh proteins.

C – AVOID: Red wine is high in tyramine. All alcohol is risky; wine and tap beer especially so.

D – AVOID: Cured/processed meats contain high tyramine due to fermentation.

E – SAFE: Fresh vegetables are fine — not fermented or aged.

Question 10Appropriate Response

Rationale

B – Correct. Bupropion LOWERS the seizure threshold — it is absolutely contraindicated in seizure disorders. The nurse must advocate for patient safety by notifying the provider before any administration.

Question 11Select All That Apply
A client presents with agitation, hyperthermia, diaphoresis, and clonus. The nurse suspects serotonin syndrome. Which medications in the history support this? Select all that apply.

  • D Phenelzine (Nardil)

Question 12Priority
Before giving propranolol for performance anxiety, the nurse checks VS: BP 108/70, HR 52, RR 18, SpO2 98%. What is the priority action?
  • A Administer propranolol since the BP is acceptable.
  • B Hold propranolol and notify the provider — HR is below 60 bpm.
  • C Give half the dose since the HR is slightly low.
  • D Administer and reassess HR in 30 minutes.
✓ Correct: B
Rationale

B – Correct. Standard parameter: HOLD beta-blockers if HR < 60 bpm. HR is 52. Propranolol slows the heart further — giving it to a bradycardic client risks dangerous bradycardia, heart block, or cardiac arrest.

A – Incorrect. BP acceptability does not override HR. Both must be within safe range.

D – Dangerous. Administering a medication that further slows an already bradycardic heart is potentially fatal.

Question 15Appropriate Response
A client who has taken diazepam (Valium) for 6 months stopped it cold turkey 3 days ago. Now experiencing tremors, anxiety, and restlessness. What is the nurse's most appropriate response?
  • A "Great that you want to stop — these symptoms will resolve on their own."
  • B "You need immediate evaluation. Abrupt benzo withdrawal can cause life-threatening seizures."
  • C "Push through the symptoms — they're just anxiety from your baseline."
  • D "Try melatonin and deep breathing until the symptoms pass."
✓ Correct: B
Rationale

B – Correct and Urgent. Abrupt discontinuation of benzodiazepines after long-term use (6 months) is a MEDICAL EMERGENCY. GABA receptors have adapted to chronic benzo exposure — without it, CNS excitability surges → tremors, anxiety, diaphoresis, and most dangerously: GRAND MAL SEIZURES and delirium. Benzo withdrawal can be more dangerous than opioid withdrawal. Immediate evaluation and supervised tapering protocol are required.

A – Dangerous. Normalizing symptoms dismisses a life-threatening risk. Seizures can occur days after the last dose.

C – Incorrect. These are NOT psychological symptoms — they are physiological withdrawal. Ignoring them risks seizures.

D – Incorrect. Non-pharmacological interventions are completely insufficient for benzo withdrawal in a long-term user.

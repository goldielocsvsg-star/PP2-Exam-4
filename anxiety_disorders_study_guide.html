Anxiety Disorders
The Foundation

Master the core definition, neurobiology, and physiologic response before diving into individual disorders.

🔑 What Is Anxiety?

A subjective feeling of dread or unease paired with objective physiologic changes.

It becomes a DISORDER when:
  • Persistent (not just occasional)
  • Excessive (out of proportion)
  • Impairs daily functioning

⚡ Autonomic (Sympathetic) Activation — Fight or Flight

💓 ↑ HR (tachycardia) 🩸 ↑ BP 💨 ↑ RR (tachypnea) 💧 Sweating 💪 Muscle tension
NCLEX TIP If you see autonomic symptoms → think anxiety or panic disorder

Neurotransmitter Pathophysiology

🧠 Critical Concept Anxiety = Too much excitation + Not enough inhibition
The brain needs GABA to calm down — when GABA is low, anxiety goes up.
NeurotransmitterTypeRoleEffect in AnxietyDrug Connection
GABA Inhibitory Calms the nervous system ↓ GABA = ↑ anxiety Benzos enhance GABA → calming
Serotonin Mood Regulates mood & emotion Dysregulation = anxiety + depression SSRIs/SNRIs increase serotonin
Norepinephrine Alertness Arousal, alertness, "alarm" ↑ NE = hyperarousal SNRIs also affect NE
Dopamine Reward Motivation, reward pathway Imbalance contributes to anxiety Buspirone acts on dopamine receptors

The GABA–Benzodiazepine Connection

🔬 How Benzos Work

Benzodiazepines bind to the GABA-A receptor and enhance GABA's inhibitory effect → this causes CNS depression → calming, sedation, and anxiolysis.

Why This Matters for Exams If a question asks why benzos cause respiratory depression — it's because excessive GABA enhancement depresses the brainstem respiratory centers.

Generalized Anxiety
Disorder (GAD)

Chronic, excessive worry that isn't tied to one specific trigger. Duration and functional impairment are key diagnostic criteria.

🔑 Diagnostic Criteria

  • Excessive worry about multiple topics
  • Duration: ≥ 6 months
  • Difficult to control the worry
  • Causes significant impairment
  • Not caused by substance or medical condition

🩺 Nursing Assessment Priorities

  • Assess sleep patterns (major symptom)
  • Evaluate functional impairment
  • Rule out hyperthyroidism (mimics anxiety)
  • Rule out substance use (caffeine, stimulants)
  • Assess for depression (frequent comorbidity)

Symptom Profile — High Yield

💪 Physical / Somatic

  • Muscle tension ← VERY common test point
  • Fatigue
  • Tremors
  • Restlessness, feeling "on edge"

⚡ Autonomic

  • Sweating
  • Tachycardia
  • Cold/clammy hands
  • Dry mouth

🧠 Psychological

  • Poor concentration
  • Irritability
  • Sleep disturbances
  • Mind goes blank

Treatment — GAD

🥇 Non-Pharmacologic FIRST (in real life) CBT (Cognitive Behavioral Therapy) is the gold standard. NCLEX often asks "best initial treatment" → therapy/CBT. However, pharmacologic treatment is frequently needed alongside therapy.

🧘 Non-Pharmacologic

  • CBT — gold standard psychotherapy
  • Relaxation training (deep breathing, PMR)
  • Biofeedback
  • Exercise (proven anxiolytic effect)
  • Sleep hygiene
  • Mindfulness-based stress reduction

💊 Pharmacologic Options

🥇 First line: SSRIs (paroxetine, escitalopram)
🥇 First line: SNRIs (venlafaxine, duloxetine)
🥈 Second line: Buspirone (safe, no dependence)
⚡ Acute/short-term: Benzodiazepines

Panic Disorder
High-Yield Section

Sudden, intense fear episodes that mimic heart attacks. NCLEX loves questions about this.

⚠️ NURSING PRIORITY #1 Always rule out CARDIAC cause FIRST before diagnosing/treating panic attack. Chest pain + palpitations = assess cardiac first.

😱 Key Features

  • Sudden, no warning
  • Intense terror or dread
  • Peaks in minutes
  • Resolves within 30 minutes
  • Recurrent and unexpected

🫀 Symptoms — "Looks Like a Heart Attack"

  • Chest pain
  • Palpitations (pounding heart)
  • Shortness of breath
  • Dizziness / lightheadedness
  • Feeling of doom / fear of dying
  • Numbness or tingling
  • Derealization (not feeling real)
🔥 NCLEX TIP If the stem says "patient thinks they are dying" or "feels like they are going to die" → think Panic Disorder

Treatment — Panic Disorder

🥇 First Line

SSRIs for long-term prevention and maintenance therapy.

Paroxetine, Sertraline

🥈 Acute Episodes

Benzodiazepines for immediate relief during acute attacks — short-term use only.

Alprazolam, Lorazepam

🧘 Therapy

CBT with exposure therapy to reduce anticipatory anxiety and avoidance behaviors.

Breathing retraining too

PTSD
Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder

The brain gets "stuck" in danger mode following exposure to severe trauma.

🪖 Core Concept

PTSD occurs after exposure to actual or threatened death, serious injury, or sexual violence — either directly experienced, witnessed, or learned about happening to a close person. The brain remains hypervigilant long after the danger has passed.

The Core Triad — MEMORIZE

🔄
Re-Experiencing
Flashbacks, nightmares, intrusive memories, psychological or physiologic distress when exposed to reminders
🚪
Avoidance
Avoiding trauma-related thoughts, feelings, people, places, conversations. Emotional numbing, detachment
⚡
Hyperarousal
Exaggerated startle response, sleep problems, irritability, difficulty concentrating, hypervigilance

Treatment — PTSD

💊 Pharmacologic

  • SSRIs — first line (sertraline, paroxetine FDA-approved)
  • SNRIs — venlafaxine (effective)
  • Prazosin — for nightmares specifically

🧠 Therapy (Very Important)

  • Trauma-focused CBT
  • Exposure therapy (gradual re-exposure)
  • EMDR (Eye Movement Desensitization and Reprocessing)
  • Stress Inoculation Training

Nursing Priorities — PTSD

🤝 Build Trust

Consistency, honesty, and establishing a safe therapeutic environment are foundational before any other intervention.

🚨 Avoid Triggers

Identify known trauma triggers. Avoid startling the patient. Ask before touching. Maintain calm, predictable environment.

🔍 Monitor Closely

High risk for depression and suicide. Monitor mood, screen for suicidal ideation, and ensure safety at all times.

🚨 Safety Alert PTSD patients have significantly elevated risk of suicide and self-harm. Always assess safety, monitor closely, and document any changes in mood or behavior.

OCD
Obsessive-Compulsive Disorder

A cycle of intrusive thoughts (obsessions) driving repetitive behaviors (compulsions) to relieve anxiety.

💭 Obsessions

Definition: Recurrent, persistent, intrusive, unwanted thoughts, urges, or images that cause marked anxiety.

Examples:
  • "My hands are contaminated"
  • Fear of harming others
  • Need for symmetry/order
  • Intrusive sexual or religious thoughts

🔄 Compulsions

Definition: Repetitive behaviors or mental acts performed to reduce the distress caused by obsessions.

Examples:
  • Excessive handwashing
  • Checking locks/appliances repeatedly
  • Counting, tapping, ordering
  • Mental rituals (praying, reviewing)
🔑 Key Concept The compulsion temporarily relieves the anxiety caused by the obsession — but reinforces the cycle. The patient knows the behavior is irrational but cannot stop. OCD is ego-dystonic (the thoughts feel foreign/unwanted, unlike delusions).

Treatment — OCD

💊 Pharmacologic

  • SSRIs — first line
  • Requires HIGHER doses than depression treatment
  • Clomipramine (TCA) — second line, very effective
TEST POINT OCD needs higher SSRI doses than GAD or depression

🧠 Behavioral Therapy

  • ERP: Exposure and Response Prevention — gold standard therapy
  • Gradual exposure to feared stimuli
  • Prevention of compulsive response
  • Combines with medication for best outcome

Nursing Priorities — OCD

⚠️ Do NOT Interrupt Compulsions Abruptly

Abruptly stopping a compulsion causes extreme anxiety and distress. Work with the patient gradually to reduce behaviors through behavioral therapy — never forcefully stop.

🕐 Allow Time for Rituals (Initially)

Acknowledge the behavior without judgment. Allow extra time for ADLs. Gradually work toward reduction through structured therapy, not abrupt interruption.

Benzodiazepines
High-Priority Drug Class

The most tested anxiolytic class. Know the mechanism, risks, withdrawal signs, and nursing priorities cold.

🚨 THIS SECTION IS EXTREMELY HIGH-YIELD FOR NCLEX Benzodiazepines appear frequently in NCLEX questions testing pharmacology knowledge. Master every detail here.

Mechanism of Action

🔬 How Benzos Work

Benzodiazepines enhance the effect of GABA (the brain's main inhibitory neurotransmitter) by binding to the GABA-A receptor complex. This increases chloride ion influx → hyperpolarization → CNS depression.

Result Sedation · Anxiolysis · Muscle relaxation · Anticonvulsant effects · Respiratory depression at high doses

Major Risks — Memorize All

🫁 Respiratory Depression Watch RR — if <12 breaths/min → DANGER. Benzos suppress brainstem respiratory drive. Additive with opioids and alcohol.
🔄 Physical Dependence Can develop with regular use. Patient will experience withdrawal if stopped abruptly. ALWAYS TAPER.
⚠️ Withdrawal Symptoms — SEVERE Anxiety rebound · Tremors · Insomnia · Seizures (can be LIFE-THREATENING)

This is why you NEVER stop benzos abruptly!
😴 Sedation & Falls CNS depression causes sedation → HIGH fall risk. Always institute fall precautions.

SSRIs & SNRIs
First-Line Long-Term Therapy

The backbone of anxiety disorder pharmacotherapy. Slow onset but the safest long-term option.

SSRIs — Selective Serotonin Reuptake Inhibitors

🔬 Mechanism

Block serotonin reuptake pumps in the presynaptic neuron → serotonin accumulates in the synapse → greater receptor activation → improved mood and reduced anxiety over time.

💊 Key Examples

• Paroxetine (Paxil) — GAD, panic, OCD, PTSD, social
• Escitalopram (Lexapro) — GAD, depression
• Sertraline (Zoloft) — PTSD, panic, OCD
• Fluoxetine (Prozac) — OCD, panic, depression

Critical SSRI Points (Highly Tested)

⏳ ONSET — MOST TESTED SSRI FACT Initial effect: ~1 week (sleep, energy may improve)
Full therapeutic effect: 4–6 weeks

NCLEX trap: Patient stops early because "it's not working" → EDUCATE: takes weeks to work!
🚨 BLACK BOX WARNING Increased risk of suicidal ideation in children, adolescents, and young adults (under 25). Monitor closely especially in the first few weeks. Paradoxically, as energy returns before mood lifts, risk increases.
⚠️ Major Side Effects (Know All)
  • GI upset (nausea — very common at start)
  • Insomnia or sleep changes
  • Sexual dysfunction ← VERY tested
  • Weight changes (gain or loss)
  • Headache
  • Serotonin syndrome (with MAOIs)
⚠️ SEROTONIN SYNDROME — RECOGNIZE IT Caused by: SSRI + MAOI combination (or other serotonergic drugs)
Signs: Hyperthermia · Agitation · Muscle rigidity · Tremors · Clonus · Diaphoresis · Confusion
Action: STOP medication immediately, supportive care, cyproheptadine (antidote)

SNRIs — Serotonin-Norepinephrine Reuptake Inhibitors

🔬 Mechanism

Block both serotonin AND norepinephrine reuptake. Dual action can be more effective for certain patients and conditions (like pain + anxiety comorbidity).

⚠️ KEY SIDE EFFECT — HYPERTENSION

The norepinephrine component causes elevated blood pressure. This is the most-tested SNRI side effect.

NURSING ACTION Monitor BP regularly on SNRIs. Report significant increases to provider.

Buspirone
The "Different" Anxiolytic

BuSpar is uniquely different from all other anxiolytics — NCLEX loves testing its comparison to benzos.

🌟 WHY BUSPIRONE IS SPECIAL It is the ONLY anxiolytic with no abuse potential, no dependence, no sedation, and no respiratory depression risk. This makes it ideal for patients with substance abuse history or those needing long-term management without addiction risk.

Side Effects

💫 Dizziness

Most common side effect. Teach patient to rise slowly (orthostatic precautions).

🤕 Headache

Common, especially at initiation. Usually resolves with continued therapy.

🤢 Nausea

GI upset at start. Taking with food can help reduce nausea.

Drug Interactions — Buspirone

🍊 GRAPEFRUIT JUICE — AVOID Grapefruit inhibits CYP3A4 enzymes → increases buspirone blood levels → increased side effects. TEACH: Do not take with grapefruit juice.
⚠️ CYP3A4 Inhibitors Erythromycin (antibiotic) and Ketoconazole (antifungal) both inhibit CYP3A4 → increase buspirone levels. Dose adjustment may be needed.

NCLEX Traps & Tips
Don't Get Fooled

The most common ways students lose points on anxiety disorder questions — and how to avoid them.

Top NCLEX Traps

❌
Trap #1: "This medication works immediately" WRONG for SSRIs, SNRIs, and Buspirone. Only benzos work immediately. SSRIs take 4–6 weeks for full effect. Students often select "the patient reports feeling better after 2 days" as an expected outcome — that is UNEXPECTED for SSRIs.
❌
Trap #2: "Stop the benzodiazepine abruptly" NEVER stop benzos suddenly → causes rebound anxiety, tremors, and SEIZURES. Always taper. Any answer suggesting abrupt discontinuation is WRONG.
❌
Trap #3: "Buspirone works fast / can be used PRN" WRONG. Buspirone takes 2–6 weeks. It is NOT for acute panic or PRN use. It must be taken consistently every day to build up therapeutic effect.
❌
Trap #4: "Mix benzodiazepines with alcohol or opioids" DEADLY combination. Both CNS depressants → synergistic effect → fatal respiratory depression. ALWAYS teach patients to avoid alcohol while on benzos.
❌
Trap #5: "Treat the panic attack before ruling out cardiac cause" WRONG. Always rule out MI/cardiac cause first when a patient presents with chest pain, palpitations, and SOB — even if it sounds like panic disorder.
❌
Trap #6: "Interrupt the OCD patient's compulsions" WRONG. Never abruptly interrupt compulsions → causes extreme anxiety. Allow the ritual; work with behavioral therapy gradually.
❌
Trap #7: "SSRIs are safe to stop anytime" WRONG. SSRIs should NOT be stopped abruptly — causes discontinuation syndrome (flu-like symptoms, dizziness, "brain zaps"). Always taper.
❌
Trap #8: "Young patients on SSRIs — suicide risk is gone" WRONG. Black box warning: SSRIs increase suicide risk in patients under 25 — especially in the FIRST FEW WEEKS. Monitor closely; increased energy + unchanged mood = highest risk window.

NCLEX Practice Questions
Exam Prep

Select All That Apply · Appropriate/Inappropriate Response · Expected/Unexpected Outcome · Priority Questions

Q1
Select All That Apply
A nurse is caring for a patient newly prescribed sertraline (Zoloft) for generalized anxiety disorder. Which statements, if made by the patient, indicate an understanding of the teaching? Select all that apply.
A"I should expect to feel completely better within 3–4 days of starting."
B"I should not stop this medication abruptly without talking to my doctor."
C"It may take 4–6 weeks before I notice the full therapeutic benefit."
D"I should report any new thoughts of self-harm or suicide immediately."
E"I can have a glass of wine at dinner since this is not a benzo."
✅ Correct: B, C, D

B — Correct: SSRIs should never be stopped abruptly — discontinuation syndrome can occur (flu-like symptoms, "brain zaps," dizziness).

C — Correct: Full therapeutic effect takes 4–6 weeks. Initial improvement in sleep and energy may be seen around 1 week, but mood regulation requires weeks.

D — Correct: The black box warning on SSRIs is for suicidal ideation, especially in patients under 25. The patient must report any new suicidal thoughts immediately.

A — INCORRECT: Feeling better in 3–4 days is unrealistic for SSRIs. This is a common NCLEX trap — SSRIs take weeks, not days.

E — INCORRECT: Although SSRIs are not CNS depressants like benzos, alcohol can worsen depression, anxiety, and increase side effect risk. Alcohol should be avoided.
Q2
Expected / Unexpected Outcome
A patient has been taking lorazepam (Ativan) 1 mg PO TID for 3 weeks for anxiety. The provider discontinues the medication and writes "stop lorazepam today." Which outcome would the nurse identify as UNEXPECTED and require immediate action?
AThe patient reports feeling more anxious than before starting the medication.
BThe patient develops mild insomnia over the next two nights.
CThe patient experiences a generalized tonic-clonic seizure 24 hours after stopping.
DThe patient becomes irritable and requests to speak with their doctor.
✅ Correct Answer: C — Generalized tonic-clonic seizure

This is the UNEXPECTED and dangerous outcome that requires immediate action. Abrupt discontinuation of benzodiazepines in patients who have been taking them regularly can cause life-threatening withdrawal seizures.

Why A, B, D are expected (though concerning): Rebound anxiety, insomnia, and irritability are all anticipated benzodiazepine withdrawal symptoms — unpleasant but not immediately life-threatening.

Key Nursing Action: The nurse should hold the order, contact the provider, and advocate for a tapering schedule. This order as written is unsafe. Benzos must always be tapered, not stopped abruptly.
Q3
Appropriate / Not Appropriate Response
A nurse is educating a patient newly prescribed buspirone (BuSpar) for chronic anxiety. The patient says, "When I feel really anxious, I'll just take an extra dose." Which response by the nurse is MOST appropriate?
A"That's fine — buspirone is very safe and has no abuse potential, so an extra dose won't harm you."
B"Buspirone is not effective for acute anxiety. It must be taken consistently every day and requires 2–6 weeks to reach full effect. You should contact your provider for a plan to manage acute episodes."
C"You can take buspirone as needed — just don't take more than three doses per day."
D"Since buspirone is slow-acting, you should switch to a benzodiazepine for better control."
✅ Correct Answer: B

Buspirone is NOT a PRN (as-needed) medication. It works by gradually modulating serotonin receptors and requires consistent daily dosing over 2–6 weeks to reach therapeutic levels. Taking an extra dose during acute anxiety will not provide immediate relief.

A — Wrong: While safety is accurate, telling the patient "that's fine" to use it PRN is incorrect nursing guidance.

C — Wrong: Buspirone is not a PRN drug under any circumstance.

D — Wrong: Switching to a benzodiazepine is not within the nurse's scope to recommend, and is not appropriate without provider order. The nurse's role is education, not prescribing.
Q4
Select All That Apply
A nurse is assessing a patient admitted for generalized anxiety disorder. Which findings are consistent with the diagnosis? Select all that apply.
APatient reports excessive worry about multiple areas of life for the past 8 months.
BPatient says they cannot control the worry, even when they know it is excessive.
CPatient reports muscle tension and difficulty sleeping.
DPatient reports sudden episode of chest pain, palpitations, and fear of dying 15 minutes ago that has now resolved.
EPatient has stopped attending work due to worry about performance.
✅ Correct: A, B, C, E

A — Correct: GAD requires excessive worry about multiple topics for ≥6 months. 8 months meets criteria.

B — Correct: A hallmark of GAD is the inability to control the worry, even when recognized as excessive.

C — Correct: Muscle tension and sleep disturbance are two of the six associated symptoms required for GAD diagnosis.

D — INCORRECT: This description (sudden onset, peaks in minutes, resolves in 15 min, fear of dying) is classic for a panic attack/panic disorder, NOT GAD. GAD involves persistent chronic worry, not discrete attacks.

E — Correct: Functional impairment (unable to attend work) is required for the GAD diagnosis.
Q5
Priority / Which Response is Appropriate
A patient comes to the emergency department with chest pain, shortness of breath, dizziness, and tells the nurse "I feel like I'm dying." The patient has a history of panic disorder. What is the nurse's PRIORITY action?
AObtain an ECG and cardiac enzymes to rule out myocardial infarction before addressing panic disorder.
BAdminister the patient's prescribed lorazepam and provide reassurance that this is a panic attack.
CAsk the patient to breathe into a paper bag to correct hyperventilation.
DPlace the patient in a quiet room and use therapeutic communication to reduce anxiety first.
✅ Correct Answer: A — Rule out cardiac cause FIRST

This is the classic NCLEX priority trap for panic disorder. Even though the patient has a known history of panic disorder and the symptoms are classic for a panic attack, the nurse must ALWAYS rule out a life-threatening cardiac cause first. Chest pain = cardiac workup before psychiatric assumptions.

B — Wrong: Administering lorazepam before ruling out a cardiac cause is premature and potentially dangerous.

C — Wrong: Paper bag rebreathing is outdated and potentially harmful — can worsen hypoxia. It is no longer recommended.

D — Wrong: Therapeutic communication is important but is not the priority when physical safety is unestablished.
Q6
Select All That Apply
A patient is being discharged with a prescription for lorazepam (Ativan) 0.5 mg PO BID PRN anxiety. Which discharge teaching points should the nurse include? Select all that apply.
AAvoid driving or operating heavy machinery while taking this medication.
BDo not drink alcohol or take other CNS depressants while on lorazepam.
CIf you miss doses for several days, you can restart the full dose immediately.
DLong-term use can lead to physical dependence; use as prescribed and discuss with your doctor before stopping.
EFall precautions are important, especially in older adults, due to the sedating effects.
✅ Correct: A, B, D, E

A — Correct: Lorazepam causes sedation and impairs reaction time — no driving or operating machinery.

B — Correct: Benzos + alcohol = synergistic CNS depression → potentially fatal respiratory depression.

D — Correct: Benzodiazepine dependence is a real risk with regular use. Patients should never stop without provider guidance (taper needed).

E — Correct: Sedation greatly increases fall risk, especially in elderly patients. Fall prevention education is essential.

C — INCORRECT: After a prolonged break, the patient may have lower tolerance. Restarting at full dose without provider guidance could cause respiratory depression. Always consult the provider.
Q7
Expected / Unexpected Outcome
A patient with PTSD is admitted to the inpatient psychiatric unit. The nurse is developing a care plan. Which nursing action is MOST appropriate for this patient?
AAssign a new nurse to care for this patient each shift to provide varied interaction styles.
BEncourage the patient to discuss the details of the trauma in group therapy on day one.
CAssign consistent nursing staff and establish a safe, predictable environment while building therapeutic trust gradually.
DPlace the patient in the most active area of the unit to provide stimulation and reduce hyperarousal.
✅ Correct Answer: C — Consistency, safety, and gradual trust building

PTSD patients require a safe, predictable, consistent environment. Trust is the foundation of therapeutic care. The nurse-patient relationship must be built slowly and carefully — the brain of a PTSD patient is hypervigilant and perceives threats everywhere.

A — Wrong: Rotating staff creates inconsistency and unpredictability — highly problematic for PTSD.

B — Wrong: Forcing trauma discussion too early before trust is established can cause re-traumatization and severe distress.

D — Wrong: A busy, stimulating environment would worsen hyperarousal symptoms. PTSD patients need calm, low-stimulation environments.
Q8
Which Response is NOT Appropriate
A nurse observes a patient with OCD spending 45 minutes repeatedly washing their hands before breakfast. Which nursing response is LEAST appropriate?
AAllow the patient extra time to complete morning care routines without interruption.
BObserve the behavior without judgment and document frequency and duration.
CTell the patient to stop washing their hands immediately, as breakfast is ready and the behavior is unnecessary.
DCollaborate with the treatment team to gradually incorporate exposure and response prevention therapy.
✅ Least Appropriate (Wrong Answer): C — Telling the patient to stop immediately

Abruptly interrupting compulsions causes extreme anxiety and distress in OCD patients. The compulsion serves to temporarily relieve the overwhelming anxiety caused by the obsession. Forcing cessation without therapeutic support is counterproductive and harmful.

A — Appropriate: Allowing time shows respect and reduces situational stressors.

B — Appropriate: Non-judgmental observation and documentation is correct therapeutic nursing.

D — Appropriate: ERP (Exposure and Response Prevention) with the treatment team is the gold-standard approach — gradual, not abrupt.

Select All That Apply — Pharmacology
A nurse is reviewing medications for a patient who takes diazepam (Valium) for anxiety. Which medications or substances, if reported by the patient, require the nurse to intervene? Select all that apply.
AHydrocodone (opioid pain medication) prescribed by another provider.
BDaily multivitamin supplement.

DValerian root tea for sleep (herbal supplement).

✅ Correct — Intervene for: A, C, D

A — Intervene: Opioids + benzodiazepines = additive CNS/respiratory depression. This combination is the leading cause of overdose deaths. Must notify provider immediately.

C — Intervene: Alcohol + benzodiazepines = dangerously synergistic CNS depression → can cause fatal respiratory depression. Patient must be educated and this must be reported.

B — No Intervention Needed: Multivitamins have no significant interaction with diazepam.

E — No Intervention Needed: Lisinopril (ACE inhibitor) does not interact with benzodiazepines in a clinically significant way.
Q10
Appropriate Response — Therapeutic Communication
A patient experiencing an acute panic attack tells the nurse, "I can't breathe! My heart is racing! I'm having a heart attack — I'm going to die!" Which response by the nurse is MOST therapeutic?
A"You are not having a heart attack. This is just a panic attack. You will be fine in a few minutes."
B"I can see you're very frightened. I'm staying right here with you. Let's breathe together slowly — in through your nose, out through your mouth."
C"Try to calm down. Your vitals are normal and there is nothing wrong with you physically."
D"These feelings are all in your head. Panic attacks are not real medical emergencies."
✅ Correct Answer: B — Acknowledge, presence, guided breathing

This response demonstrates the three pillars of therapeutic panic management: (1) Validate the feeling ("I can see you're very frightened"), (2) Presence and safety ("I'm staying right here with you"), and (3) Guided breathing to activate the parasympathetic nervous system and reduce sympathetic activation.

A — Wrong: Dismissing the patient's experience ("just a panic attack") is invalidating and increases anxiety. Also — cardiac must still be ruled out.

C — Wrong: Telling a terrified patient to "calm down" is ineffective and dismissive. It invalidates their experience.

D — Wrong: Calling the symptoms "all in your head" is deeply invalidating, inaccurate, and therapeutically harmful. Panic attacks are real physiologic events.

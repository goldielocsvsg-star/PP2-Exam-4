Slides 3–10 | Lehne Chapter 34 Introduction

Neurotransmitters — Function & Imbalance

📘 Why This Matters for Pharmacology

Every antipsychotic drug in this module works by correcting a neurotransmitter imbalance. Understanding how NTs are released, transmitted, and cleared — and what happens when this goes wrong — is the foundation for understanding WHY these drugs work and WHY they have the side effects they do.

Slide 3 — Neurotransmitter Function (The Process)
📊 Slide Content
  • Step 1: Impulse arrives at axon terminal
  • Step 2: Neurotransmitter (NT) is released from vesicles into the synaptic cleft
  • Step 3: NT binds to receptor on postsynaptic membrane
  • Step 4: Causes an action (excitation or inhibition)
📖 Textbook Expansion — Synaptic Transmission

The presynaptic neuron stores NTs in small membrane sacs called vesicles located in the axon terminal. When a nerve impulse (action potential) travels down the axon and reaches the terminal, it triggers these vesicles to fuse with the presynaptic membrane and release NTs into the synaptic cleft — the tiny gap between two neurons. The NT then diffuses across the cleft and binds to specific receptor proteins on the postsynaptic membrane, triggering a new action potential (excitatory) or suppressing one (inhibitory).

Slide 4 — What Happens to the NT After Release
📊 Slide Content
  • A released NT can be: (1) taken back up into the neuron through reuptake, (2) diffused out of the synaptic cleft, or (3) broken down by enzymes into inactive substances or metabolites
  • Example: acetylcholine is rapidly broken down by the enzyme acetylcholinesterase
  • Faulty release, reuptake, or elimination of NTs may lead to IMBALANCE
📖 Textbook Expansion — Reuptake Is the Primary Termination Mechanism

For most NTs (especially norepinephrine), the predominant method of termination is reuptake — the NT is taken back into the presynaptic neuron in unchanged form and repackaged for reuse. This is why SSRIs (reuptake inhibitors) are effective antidepressants — they block this reuptake process, leaving more serotonin available in the synapse.

NT imbalance is the biochemical basis of psychiatric disorders. Understanding the three clearance mechanisms helps explain drug mechanisms: antidepressants block reuptake, MAOIs block enzymatic breakdown, and antipsychotics block receptor binding.

Slide 7 — Neurotransmitter GABA (In Detail)
📊 Slide Content
  • Major neurotransmitter in psychopathology
  • Associated with the regulation of ANXIETY
  • Too little GABA = anxiety
  • Example: Benzodiazepines (antianxiety meds) bind to GABA receptor site → making receptor more sensitive to GABA (potentiating GABA) → decreased anxiety
📖 Textbook Expansion — GABA as the Brain's Main "Brake"

GABA (gamma-aminobutyric acid) is the major inhibitory neurotransmitter in the CNS. When GABA binds to its receptor, it opens chloride channels, which hyperpolarizes the neuron and makes it harder to fire. Benzodiazepines do NOT mimic GABA — they bind to a nearby allosteric site on the GABA-A receptor complex, making the receptor more sensitive to GABA (increasing the frequency of chloride channel opening). This is why benzodiazepines require GABA to be present — they potentiate, not replace, GABA activity.

⭐ Exam Tip — GABA in This Module

Benzodiazepines (clonazepam, lorazepam, diazepam) appear in Module 8 (Bipolar) as adjunct therapy for acute mania/insomnia/agitation. They also appear in EPS management. Know that their mechanism is GABA potentiation — stimulating inhibitory GABA receptors → sedative, anti-anxiety, and muscle relaxant effects.

Slide 8 — Neurotransmitter Serotonin (In Detail)
📊 Slide Content
  • Regulates: sleep, wakefulness, and mood; delusions & hallucinations
  • Associated with arousal and CNS activity levels
  • Too little = depression; too much = delusions & hallucinations
  • Antidepressants block the reuptake of serotonin into the presynaptic neuron
  • Examples: SSRIs (selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors), MAOIs (older drugs with many SE)
📖 Textbook Expansion — Serotonin's Dual Role

Serotonin (5-hydroxytryptamine, 5-HT) is produced primarily in the raphe nuclei of the brainstem and projects widely throughout the brain. It plays a role in mood, sleep, appetite, memory, and social behavior. In psychosis, excessive serotonin activity at 5-HT2A receptors in the prefrontal cortex and limbic system contributes to delusions and hallucinations. This is why SGAs (atypical antipsychotics) block both D2 receptors AND 5-HT2A receptors — the serotonin blockade reduces psychotic symptoms while also reducing the EPS burden caused by strong D2 blockade alone.

Slide 9 — Neurotransmitter Dopamine (In Detail)
📊 Slide Content
  • Regulates cognition, emotional responses, and motivation
  • Associated with: schizophrenia and other psychoses (too much dopamine); Parkinson's disease (too little dopamine + increased acetylcholine)
  • Example: antipsychotic drugs block dopamine receptors → helps clients organize thoughts and respond appropriately
📖 Textbook Expansion — The Four Dopamine Pathways

Dopamine acts through four major pathways in the brain, and understanding them explains BOTH the therapeutic effects AND side effects of antipsychotics:

  • Mesolimbic pathway: Overactive in schizophrenia → responsible for positive symptoms (hallucinations, delusions). Blocking D2 here = therapeutic benefit.
  • Mesocortical pathway: Underactive in schizophrenia → responsible for negative and cognitive symptoms. This is why FGAs (strong D2 blockers) worsen negative symptoms.
  • Nigrostriatal pathway: Controls motor function — same as the pathway impaired in Parkinson's disease. Blocking D2 here = EPS (drug-induced parkinsonism, dystonia, akathisia, TD).
  • Tuberoinfundibular pathway: Controls prolactin release. Blocking D2 here = elevated prolactin → gynecomastia, galactorrhea, menstrual irregularities.
⭐ Critical Exam Connection — Dopamine and EPS

EPS occurs because antipsychotics block D2 receptors in the nigrostriatal pathway — the same pathway damaged in Parkinson's disease. This is why drug-induced parkinsonism looks identical to idiopathic PD (bradykinesia, rigidity, shuffling gait, masked facies). Anticholinergics (benztropine) treat EPS by restoring the dopamine/acetylcholine balance in this pathway.

Slide 10 — Neurotransmitter Norepinephrine (In Detail)
📊 Slide Content
  • Involved with thinking, planning, and interpreting
  • Associated with control of arousal, attention, vigilance, mood, affect, and anxiety
  • Too much = psychosis; too little = depression
  • Tricyclic antidepressants block the reuptake of NE to treat depressive disorders
  • Note: NE action is largely terminated by the reuptake process — taken up in unchanged form and reused
📖 Textbook Expansion — NE and Antipsychotic Side Effects

Norepinephrine (NE) is produced in the locus coeruleus and projects throughout the brain. In the context of antipsychotic pharmacology, the key connection is the alpha-1 adrenergic receptor on blood vessels. Antipsychotics block alpha-1 receptors → prevent compensatory vasoconstriction when standing → blood pools in the legs → BP drops → orthostatic hypotension. This is more pronounced with low-potency FGAs (chlorpromazine) than with high-potency agents (haloperidol).

Schizophrenia — Clinical Presentation & Etiology

Slide 11 — Antipsychotic Agents: Scope of Use
📊 Slide Content
  • Used for a diverse spectrum of psychotic disorders
  • Specific indications: schizophrenia, delusional disorders, bipolar disorders, depressive psychosis, and drug-induced psychoses
  • Should NOT be used to treat dementia in the older adult
📖 Textbook Expansion

The reason antipsychotics are contraindicated for dementia-related psychosis in older adults is that all antipsychotics (FGAs and SGAs) approximately double the rate of mortality in this population. Most deaths result from heart-related events (heart failure, sudden death) or from infection (mainly pneumonia). This warning applies to both drug generations and is a Black Box Warning on all antipsychotic medications.

Additional uses mentioned in the textbook (not all on the slide): Tourette syndrome, suppression of emesis (especially phenothiazines like chlorpromazine), Huntington's chorea, and control of severe behavioral problems in children (haloperidol — as a last resort only).

⚠️ Nursing Priority — Age-Related Caution

If you see an order for an antipsychotic drug for an elderly patient diagnosed with dementia, this is a medication to question. Antipsychotics are not approved for this use and carry a mortality risk. Alert the prescriber and document your concern.

Slide 12 — FGAs vs. SGAs Introduction
📊 Slide Content
  • First-generation antipsychotics (FGAs) or conventional antipsychotics: block receptors for dopamine in the CNS; cause serious movement disorders known as extrapyramidal symptoms (EPS)
  • Second-generation antipsychotics (SGAs) or atypical antipsychotics: produce only moderate blockade of dopamine receptors; stronger blockade for serotonin; fewer EPS
📖 Textbook Expansion — FGAs and SGAs Are Equally Effective

A critical finding from two large government-sponsored studies (one US, one UK): FGAs and SGAs are equally effective for treating schizophrenia. Despite initial impressions that SGAs were superior, this has not held up. The SGAs simply exchanged one set of serious side effects (EPS) for another (metabolic effects: weight gain, diabetes, dyslipidemia). FGAs cost 10–20 times less than SGAs. The choice between them is driven by individual patient factors — not by superior efficacy of one class over the other.

The one exception: Clozapine (SGA) is genuinely more effective than all other antipsychotics and is reserved for treatment-resistant cases.

Slides 13–15 — Schizophrenia: Three Types of Symptoms
📊 Slide Content — Positive Symptoms (Slide 13)
  • Disordered thinking and reduced ability to comprehend reality
  • Positive symptoms = exaggeration or distortion of normal function
  • Hallucinations, delusions, agitation, tension, paranoia
📊 Slide Content — Negative Symptoms (Slide 14)
  • Negative symptoms = loss or diminution of normal function
  • Lack of motivation, poverty of speech, blunted affect, poor self-care, social withdrawal
📊 Slide Content — Cognitive Symptoms (Slide 15)
  • Disordered thinking, reduced ability to focus attention, prominent learning and memory difficulties
  • Subtle changes may appear years before symptoms become florid
  • Florid changes: thinking and speech may be completely incomprehensible to others
📖 Textbook Expansion — Symptom Responsiveness to Treatment

Positive symptoms may respond somewhat better than negative symptoms to antipsychotic treatment. FGAs (strong D2 blockers) are most effective against positive symptoms. However, because positive and negative symptoms both respond to FGAs and SGAs, this distinction matters most in the following clinical scenario:

  • A patient who remains socially withdrawn, unmotivated, and flat after FGA treatment may have residual negative symptoms that require switching to an SGA
  • Clozapine is the only antipsychotic consistently shown to improve positive, negative, AND cognitive symptoms
  • Cognitive symptoms respond equally to FGAs and SGAs

Slide 16 — Acute Episodes vs. Residual Symptoms
📊 Slide Content
  • Acute episodes: delusions (fixed false beliefs) and hallucinations are frequently prominent
  • Residual symptoms: suspiciousness; poor anxiety management; diminished judgment, insight, motivation, and capacity for self-care
📖 Textbook Expansion — Acute vs. Residual Phase Nursing Implications

During an acute episode, delusions are typically religious, grandiose, or persecutory. Auditory hallucinations are more common than visual. The patient may feel controlled by external forces. Misperception of reality may produce hostility and non-cooperation. Impaired self-care leaves the patient disheveled; sleep and eating are disrupted.

During residual phases, even though florid symptoms (overt delusions, hallucinations) have subsided, patients frequently cannot establish close relationships, maintain employment, or function independently. Suspiciousness and poor anxiety management → social withdrawal. Inability to appreciate the need for ongoing drug therapy → non-adherence → relapse. This is why patient education is a top nursing priority even during remission.

Slide 17 — Long-Term Course & Etiology
📊 Slide Content
  • Long-term course: acute exacerbations with periods of remission
  • Etiology: exact cause is unknown
  • Contributing factors: genetic, perinatal, neurodevelopmental, neuroanatomic
  • Possible primary defects: excessive activation of CNS receptors for dopamine; insufficient activation of CNS receptors for glutamate
📖 Textbook Expansion — Etiology and Course

Although schizophrenia has a strong biologic basis, the exact etiology remains unknown. The dopamine hypothesis (excessive dopamine activity = positive symptoms) and the glutamate hypothesis (insufficient NMDA receptor activity = negative and cognitive symptoms) are the dominant current theories. Psychosocial stressors can precipitate acute exacerbations in susceptible patients but are not considered causative.

As years pass, some patients experience progressive mental and social decline; others stabilize or improve. Maintenance antipsychotic therapy reduces relapse risk but may fail to prevent long-term deterioration. Antipsychotics treat symptoms — they do NOT alter the underlying disease pathology and are NOT curative.

First-Generation Antipsychotics — Classification & Mechanism

Slide 18 — FGA Overview (What the Slide Covers)
📊 Slide Content — Topics Listed
  • Classification | Mechanism of action | Therapeutic uses | Adverse effects
  • Physical and psychologic dependence | Drug interactions | Toxicity
📘 Context — Why Start With FGAs?

The textbook notes that FGA pharmacology is well understood because they have been in use since the 1950s. Understanding FGAs gives you the foundation to understand SGAs, since their pharmacology is very similar. The main differences between FGAs and SGAs are the degree of D2 vs. serotonin blockade and the resulting side effect profile differences.

Slide 19 — Classification
📊 Slide Content
  • Chemical classification: phenothiazines and butyrophenones
  • Phenothiazine prototype: chlorpromazine
  • Butyrophenone prototype: haloperidol
  • Drugs in all groups are equivalent with respect to antipsychotic actions
📖 Textbook Expansion — Classification by Potency Is More Clinically Useful

While chemical classification (phenothiazines vs. butyrophenones) describes molecular structure, classification by potency is more useful clinically because it predicts side effects.

  • High-potency FGAs (e.g., haloperidol): smaller doses needed → more early EPS, but less sedation, less orthostatic hypotension, fewer anticholinergic effects → generally preferred for initial therapy
  • Low-potency FGAs (e.g., chlorpromazine): larger doses needed → less early EPS, but more sedation, more orthostatic hypotension, more anticholinergic effects
  • Tardive dyskinesia risk = EQUAL for ALL FGAs regardless of potency — this is a critical exam point
⭐ Exam Trap — Potency ≠ Efficacy

When we say haloperidol is "high potency," we mean a smaller dose is needed — NOT that it produces better outcomes. When administered in therapeutically equivalent doses, all FGAs produce equivalent antipsychotic effects. Potency only predicts which side effects are more likely.

Slide 20 — Therapeutic Uses
📊 Slide Content
  • Schizophrenia is the primary indication for antipsychotic drugs
  • These drugs suppress symptoms during acute psychotic episodes
  • Continued use reduces the risk for relapse
📖 Textbook Expansion — Three Treatment Goals + Timeline

The three major objectives of antipsychotic drug therapy are: (1) suppression of acute episodes, (2) prevention of acute exacerbations, and (3) maintenance of the highest possible level of functioning. Regarding timeline:

  • Initial effects may be seen in 1–2 days
  • Significant improvement typically takes 1–2 weeks
  • Full effects may not develop for several months
  • 50% of patients who haven't responded by 6 weeks will respond by 12 weeks

Important: Antipsychotics do NOT alter the underlying pathology of schizophrenia. They provide symptomatic relief only and are NOT curative. Patients who feel "cured" must be strongly encouraged to continue therapy.

Slide 21 — Mechanism of Action
📊 Slide Content
  • Conventional antipsychotic drugs block a variety of receptors within and outside of the CNS
  • Suppress symptoms of psychosis by blocking dopamine₂ receptors in the brain
  • Adverse effects are a result of blocking receptors for dopamine, acetylcholine, histamine, and norepinephrine

⭐ Exam Tip — Connect the Receptor to the Side Effect

This mechanism table is the foundation for understanding EVERY antipsychotic side effect. If a question asks why a patient on chlorpromazine became dizzy upon standing → alpha-1 blockade → orthostatic hypotension. Why is the patient sedated? → H1 blockade. Slides 22–26 | EPS: Dystonia, Parkinsonism, Akathisia, Tardive Dyskinesia

FGAs — Extrapyramidal Symptoms (EPS)

Slide 22 — EPS Overview
📊 Slide Content
  • EPS = Extrapyramidal Symptoms — same neuronal network as Parkinson's disease (blockade of D2 receptors)
  • Early in therapy: acute dystonia, parkinsonism, and akathisia
  • Late in therapy: tardive dyskinesia (TD)
📖 Textbook Expansion — Why EPS Looks Like Parkinson's

The extrapyramidal system is the neuronal network responsible for coordinating motor movement — the same network disrupted in Parkinson's disease. FGAs block D2 receptors in the nigrostriatal pathway of this system. Because idiopathic PD also results from reduced dopamine in this pathway, drug-induced parkinsonism and idiopathic PD share the same symptoms — they are indistinguishable clinically.

Early EPS (dystonia, parkinsonism, akathisia) are more frequent with high-potency FGAs. However, the risk for tardive dyskinesia is equal with ALL FGAs regardless of potency — high-potency FGAs do NOT have a lower TD risk.

Slide 23 — Acute Dystonia
📊 Slide Content
  • Involuntary muscular contractions, twisting of body parts, repetitive movements, increased muscular tone, rhythmic jerks
  • Treatment: removal of drug OR anticholinergic — benztropine or diphenhydramine
📖 Textbook Expansion — Timing, Severity, and Differentiation

Acute dystonia typically develops within the first few hours to 5 days of therapy. The patient develops severe spasm of the muscles of the tongue, face, neck, or back. Oculogyric crisis (involuntary upward eye deviation) and opisthotonus (tetanic spasm of back muscles — trunk arches forward while head and lower limbs thrust backward) may occur. Laryngeal dystonia can impair respiration.

Treatment is urgent: anticholinergic IM or IV. IV benztropine resolves symptoms within 5 minutes; IM resolves within 15–20 minutes.

Critical clinical distinction: Acute dystonia can be mistaken for psychotic hysteria. Misdiagnosis could lead to increasing the antipsychotic dose → worsening dystonia. Always consider EPS when a patient on antipsychotics suddenly develops muscle spasms or eye deviations.

⚠️ Nursing Priority — Laryngeal Dystonia

If the dystonia involves the larynx, it can compromise the airway. This is a nursing emergency. Assess respiratory status immediately and ensure emergency airway equipment is accessible. Administer anticholinergic medication as ordered and notify the provider immediately.

Slide 24 — Drug-Induced Parkinsonism
📊 Slide Content
  • Parkinsonism: bradykinesia, mask-like face, drooling, tremor, cogwheel rigidity, shuffling gait, stooped posture
  • Bradykinesia = extreme slowness of movement (brady = slow, kinesia = movement)
  • Treatment: anticholinergics — benztropine, trihexyphenidyl, diphenhydramine, or amantadine
📖 Textbook Expansion — Onset, Duration, and Management

Drug-induced parkinsonism develops within the first 5–30 days of therapy. Symptoms are indistinguishable from idiopathic Parkinson's disease. Treatment uses anticholinergic drugs (benztropine, diphenhydramine) and amantadine. Levodopa and dopamine agonists should NOT be used — they activate dopamine receptors and would counteract the antipsychotic benefit.

Unlike idiopathic PD, drug-induced parkinsonism tends to resolve spontaneously within months. Antiparkinsonism drugs should be tapered after a few months to assess whether they are still needed. For severe cases, switching to an SGA (which has lower EPS risk due to moderate D2 blockade) is effective.

Slide 25 — Akathisia
📊 Slide Content
  • Akathisia (a = without, kathis = sitting): intolerance of inactivity; motor restlessness; pacing and squirming brought on by an uncontrollable need to be in motion
  • Treatment: beta blocker, benzodiazepines, anticholinergics; OR switch to low-potency FGA
📖 Textbook Expansion — Timeline and Critical Clinical Distinction

Akathisia typically develops within 5–60 days. It is the most subjectively distressing EPS — the patient experiences profound, unrelenting restlessness that is not under voluntary control. Patients cannot sit still, pace constantly, and shift weight from foot to foot.

Critical distinction: Akathisia can be mistaken for anxiety or psychotic agitation. If misidentified, the antipsychotic dose would likely be increased — this worsens akathisia. Always consider akathisia when a patient on antipsychotics appears increasingly agitated or restless. Assessment: ask the patient if they feel an internal drive to keep moving that they cannot control.

⭐ Exam Tip — Three EPS "Confusers"
  • Acute dystonia can be mistaken for psychotic hysteria → wrong: would increase antipsychotic dose → worsens dystonia
  • Akathisia can be mistaken for psychotic agitation → wrong: would increase antipsychotic dose → worsens akathisia
  • Drug-induced parkinsonism looks identical to Parkinson's disease → distinguish by history: did symptoms start after antipsychotic initiation?
Slide 26 — Tardive Dyskinesia (TD)
📊 Slide Content
  • Twisting, worm-like movements of the tongue and face; lip-smacking; tongue flicks out in a "fly-catching" motion; slow, worm-like movement of the tongue; involuntary movements of the limbs, toes, fingers, and trunk
  • Interfere with chewing, swallowing, speaking → can result in malnutrition and weight loss
  • Treatment: reduce dose or drug withdrawal; switch to an SGA; valbenazine
📖 Textbook Expansion — Prevalence, Mechanism, and Two Approved Drugs

TD develops in approximately 5.5% of patients on long-term FGA therapy. Risk is related to duration of treatment and dose size. For many patients, symptoms are irreversible. The proposed mechanism: chronic D2 receptor blockade → receptors become supersensitive (upregulation) → when dopamine stimulates these hypersensitive receptors, it produces abnormal choreiform movements.

The textbook lists two approved treatments (vs. the slide which lists only valbenazine): valbenazine (VMAT2 inhibitor) AND deutetrabenazine (also a VMAT2 inhibitor). Neither fully eliminates TD — they only reduce symptoms. Prevention is the best approach: use the lowest effective antipsychotic dose, assess for early TD signs (fine, worm-like tongue movements) at every visit, and consider switching to an SGA at first sign of TD.

⚠️ Nursing Priority — TD Prevention and Early Detection

The AIMS (Abnormal Involuntary Movement Scale) is used to monitor for TD. Assess patients on long-term antipsychotics for early signs (subtle tongue movements, facial grimacing) at each visit. Early detection allows dose reduction before symptoms become irreversible. Teach patients and families to report any unusual face or tongue movements immediately.

Slides 27–35 | NMS, Anticholinergic Effects, Orthostatic Hypotension, Drug Interactions, Toxicity

FGAs — NMS, Additional Side Effects & Drug Interactions

Slides 27–28 — Neuroleptic Malignant Syndrome (NMS)
📊 Slide Content — Signs & Symptoms (Slide 27)
  • Rare but serious reaction; risk of death without treatment
  • "Lead pipe" muscle rigidity, sudden high fever, altered level of consciousness, sweating
  • Autonomic instability: tachycardia, dysrhythmias, fluctuations in blood pressure
  • Seizures or coma may develop; rhabdomyolysis & acute kidney failure
  • Death from respiratory failure, cardiovascular collapse, dysrhythmias
📊 Slide Content — Treatment (Slide 28)
  • Immediate withdrawal of antipsychotic medications
  • Hyperthermia: cooling blankets & antipyretics
  • Hydration: fluids
  • Benzodiazepines: muscle relaxant, relieve anxiety, reduce BP & tachycardia
  • Dantrolene: muscle relaxant, reduces rigidity & hyperthermia
  • Bromocriptine: dopamine receptor agonist, relieves CNS toxicity
🚨 EMERGENCY — NMS Is Life-Threatening

NMS carries a 5–20% mortality rate. Temperature may exceed 41°C. More likely with high-potency FGAs (e.g., haloperidol) than low-potency agents. The triad to recognize: "Lead pipe" rigidity + sudden HIGH fever + altered LOC. Any patient on antipsychotics who develops this combination = presumed NMS until proven otherwise.

Nursing action sequence: (1) STOP the antipsychotic immediately, (2) Call provider and prepare for ICU-level care, (3) Initiate cooling measures, (4) Establish IV access and start fluids, (5) Administer medications as ordered (dantrolene, bromocriptine, benzodiazepines), (6) Monitor vitals, urine output, and mental status continuously.

If the antipsychotic must be resumed after recovery: wait at least 2 weeks, use the lowest effective dose, and avoid high-potency agents.

📖 Textbook Expansion — Dantrolene and Bromocriptine Mechanisms

Dantrolene is a direct-acting muscle relaxant that reduces skeletal muscle rigidity and hyperthermia in NMS by blocking calcium release from the sarcoplasmic reticulum. Bromocriptine is a dopamine receptor agonist — it directly stimulates dopamine receptors in the CNS that were blocked by the antipsychotic, thereby relieving CNS toxicity. These two drugs work synergistically in NMS treatment.

Slide 29 — Anticholinergic Effects
📊 Slide Content
  • Anticholinergic effects: dry mouth, blurred vision, photophobia, urinary hesitancy, constipation, and tachycardia
  • Memory mnemonic (from slide 30 ANS diagram): "Can't pee, can't see, can't spit, can't sh*t"
📖 Textbook Expansion — Anticholinergic Effects Are More Common with Low-Potency FGAs

Anticholinergic effects result from blockade of muscarinic cholinergic receptors. These effects are more pronounced with low-potency FGAs (chlorpromazine) than with high-potency agents (haloperidol). Management includes: sugarless gum/frequent water sips for dry mouth; stool softeners, fluids, and fiber for constipation; voiding before dose for urinary hesitancy; sunglasses for photophobia.

Urinary hesitancy is particularly significant when combined with lithium (which causes polyuria) — this interaction is tested in the bipolar module. The textbook also notes that patients should avoid all other drugs with anticholinergic properties (antihistamines, OTC sleep aids, TCAs) to prevent additive effects.

Slide 31 — Orthostatic Hypotension
📊 Slide Content
  • Antipsychotic drugs promote orthostatic hypotension by blocking alpha₁-adrenergic receptors on blood vessels
  • This blockade prevents compensatory vasoconstriction upon standing → BP falls
  • Teach: signs of hypotension (lightheadedness, dizziness); instruct to sit or lie down; teach to get up slowly
📖 Textbook Expansion — Assessment Protocol and Tolerance

For hospitalized patients: measure BP and pulse before dosing AND 1 hour after. Take measurements while lying down and again after sitting or standing for 1–2 minutes. If BP is low or pulse is elevated, withhold the medication and consult the prescriber.

Orthostatic hypotension is more likely with low-potency FGAs and with IM dosing. Tolerance to hypotension typically develops within 2–3 months. Risk is greatest with the first dose, which is why post-dose BP monitoring is especially important at initiation of therapy.

Slide 32 — More Adverse Effects
📊 Slide Content
  • Sedation: results from histamine blocking in CNS; give at bedtime (HS)
  • Neuroendocrine effects: increased prolactin → gynecomastia (in up to 57% women; also in males)
  • Sexual dysfunction: leads to poor adherence — incidence 25–60% in men
  • Dermatologic effects: phenothiazines (chlorpromazine) → photosensitivity; contact dermatitis
  • Agranulocytosis: risk for infection; monitor WBC; withdraw medication to reverse
  • Severe dysrhythmias: FGAs prolong QT interval; check ECG and potassium
📖 Textbook Expansion — QT Prolongation and Which FGAs Are Highest Risk

Not all FGAs equally prolong the QT interval. The textbook specifically identifies four high-risk FGAs: chlorpromazine, haloperidol, thioridazine, and pimozide. QT prolongation can lead to torsades de pointes — a potentially fatal ventricular dysrhythmia. Before starting these agents: obtain a baseline ECG and serum potassium. Ensure potassium and magnesium levels are normal (hypokalemia worsens QT risk). Avoid combining with other QT-prolonging drugs (amiodarone, erythromycin, quinidine, clarithromycin).

Regarding agranulocytosis: among the FGAs, risk is highest with chlorpromazine and other phenothiazines. Check WBC whenever the patient shows signs of infection (fever, sore throat). The good news: WBC normalizes after drug withdrawal.

Regarding seizures (not on the slide but in the textbook): FGAs lower the seizure threshold. Monitor patients with seizure disorders closely; they may need increased antiseizure medication dosages.

Regarding neonatal effects: neonates exposed to antipsychotics during the 3rd trimester may experience EPS and/or withdrawal symptoms — tremor, agitation, sleepiness, feeding difficulty, severe breathing difficulty, altered muscle tone. Pregnant patients should NOT stop antipsychotics without consulting their prescriber.

Slide 33 — Physical and Psychological Dependence
📊 Slide Content
  • Development of physical and psychological dependence is rare
  • Abrupt withdrawal of antipsychotics can precipitate a mild abstinence syndrome
  • Do not stop abruptly unless medical emergency (NMS)
📖 Textbook Expansion — Withdrawal Symptoms and Tapering

Although physical dependence is minimal, abrupt withdrawal produces a mild syndrome related to chronic cholinergic blockade: restlessness, insomnia, headache, gastric distress, and sweating. The syndrome can be avoided by withdrawing FGAs gradually. Patient teaching should include: "You should never stop this medication suddenly. If you want to stop, talk to your doctor about a plan." This is especially important because patients who feel well may be tempted to stop on their own, leading to relapse.

Slide 34 — Drug Interactions
📊 Slide Content
  • Anticholinergic drugs: intensify the anticholinergic effect ("can't pee, see, spit, sh*t")
  • CNS depressants: can intensify CNS depression; avoid alcohol
  • Levodopa and direct dopamine receptor agonists: may counteract the antipsychotic effects of neuroleptics
📖 Textbook Expansion

Drugs to avoid with antipsychotics include: antihistamines, OTC sleep aids (anticholinergic), barbiturates, benzodiazepines, opioids (CNS depressants). The antihypertensive drug interaction is worth noting: both antipsychotics and antihypertensives lower BP via different mechanisms (alpha-1 blockade vs. direct vasodilation) — additive hypotension may occur.

Levodopa interaction: Levodopa activates dopamine receptors, antipsychotics block them — they directly oppose each other. Using both together reduces the benefit of each. Similarly, bromocriptine (used to treat NMS) would counteract the antipsychotic effect; its use in NMS is justified because the antipsychotic is already being withdrawn.

Slide 35 — Toxicity (Overdose)
📊 Slide Content
  • Conventional FGAs are very safe; death by overdose is extremely rare
  • Overdose produces: hypotension, CNS depression, and EPS
  • Treatment: IV fluids + alpha-adrenergic agonists (for hypotension); gastric lavage (to remove drug); antiparkinsonism drugs (for EPS)
  • No specific antidote for CNS depression
📖 Textbook Expansion

The textbook notes that with chlorpromazine, the therapeutic index is approximately 200 (lethal dose is 200 times the therapeutic dose), making FGAs very safe from a toxicology perspective. Note: Emetics (drugs that induce vomiting) cannot be used to remove the overdose because their effects would be blocked by the antiemetic action of the antipsychotic.

Slides 36–37 — Individual FGA Profiles: Haloperidol & Chlorpromazine
📊 Slide Content
  • Pharmacologic class: Butyrophenone (haloperidol) / Phenothiazine (chlorpromazine)
  • Indication: schizophrenia and acute psychosis
  • Adverse effects: EPS; NMS, orthostatic hypotension; sedation, sexual dysfunction; agranulocytosis; anticholinergic effects; dysrhythmias
  • NC: NOT recommended in older adults with dementia-related psychosis
  • Teach: SE; contraindicated in glaucoma and CV disease; caution with urinary hesitancy and constipation; tachycardia; avoid alcohol
  • Drug-drug: anticholinergics, Parkinson's drugs, antihypertensives

Slides 38–48 | Second-Generation (Atypical) Antipsychotics

Second-Generation Antipsychotics (SGAs) — Atypical Agents

Slide 39 — SGAs: Overview
📊 Slide Content
  • Introduced during the 1990s
  • Less risk of EPS than FGAs
  • Increased risk of weight gain, diabetes, and dyslipidemia (metabolic syndrome)
📖 Textbook Expansion — SGAs Did Not Live Up to Their Promise

SGAs were initially thought to be more effective AND safer than FGAs, dominating 90% of the market. Two large government studies showed that SGAs and FGAs are equally effective. The SGAs didn't eliminate serious side effects — they exchanged EPS risk for metabolic risk. FGAs cost 10–20 times less than SGAs. Clinical selection should be individualized: patients with diabetes/dyslipidemia → FGA may be better; patients with severe EPS on FGA → SGA would be better. Clozapine remains the exception — it is genuinely superior for treatment-resistant schizophrenia.

Slides 40–41 — Clozapine
📊 Slide Content — MOA and Use (Slide 40)
  • Blocks dopamine AND serotonin receptors; low affinity for D2 → fewer EPS
  • Also blocks NE, histamine, and ACh receptors
  • Therapeutic use: schizophrenia; improves positive, negative, AND cognitive symptoms
📊 Slide Content — Adverse Effects (Slide 41)
  • Metabolic syndrome: diabetes, dyslipidemia, weight gain
  • Sedation (H1 blockade), EPS, orthostatic hypotension (alpha blocking), anticholinergic effects
  • Agranulocytosis (enroll in REMS program)
  • Effects in older adults with dementia: about double the mortality rate
  • Drug-drug: anticholinergics, alcohol, antihypertensives
📖 Textbook Expansion — Clozapine REMS and Monitoring Protocol

Clozapine causes agranulocytosis in 1–2% of patients; overall mortality from agranulocytosis is ~1 in 5,000. The REMS program mandates:

  • Before starting: WBC ≥3,500/mm³ AND ANC ≥2,000/mm³ required
  • Monitoring: weekly for 6 months → biweekly months 6–12 → monthly thereafter
  • WBC <3,000/mm³ or ANC <1,500/mm³: INTERRUPT treatment
  • WBC <2,000/mm³ or ANC <1,000/mm³: PERMANENTLY DISCONTINUE; monitor counts 4 weeks post-withdrawal

Rare but important: clozapine has been associated with myocarditis (heart muscle inflammation), which can be fatal. Signs: unexplained fatigue, dyspnea, tachypnea, chest pain, palpitations. Withhold until myocarditis is ruled out; do NOT rechallenge if confirmed.

Metabolic monitoring for clozapine (and all SGAs): weight at baseline and every 3 months; waist circumference at baseline and annually; fasting blood glucose at baseline, 12 weeks, then annually; fasting lipid profile at baseline, 12 weeks, then at least every 5 years.

⚠️ Nursing Priority — Clozapine is Reserved for Treatment-Resistant Cases

Clozapine should NOT be the first antipsychotic tried. It should be reserved for patients who have not responded to safer alternatives. Once initiated, REMS enrollment and WBC monitoring are mandatory for both the prescriber and the dispensing pharmacy. Patients must be informed about agranulocytosis risk and told that clozapine will not be dispensed without proof of recent blood work.

Slides 47–48 — Depot (Long-Acting Injectable) Preparations
📊 Slide Content
  • Long-acting, injectable formulations used for long-term maintenance therapy of schizophrenia
  • No evidence that depot preparations pose an increased risk of side effects
  • Viscous liquid: use large needle (21 gauge), Z-track, deep muscle
  • Six preparations listed: haloperidol decanoate, fluphenazine decanoate, risperidone microspheres, paliperidone palmitate, aripiprazole, olanzapine pamoate
📖 Textbook Expansion — Three Advantages of Depot Therapy

Depot therapy is valuable not just for patients with adherence problems — it is beneficial for all patients needing long-term maintenance. Three advantages over oral therapy: (1) lower relapse rate with depot vs. oral; (2) more stable plasma drug levels between doses (no peaks and troughs); (3) lower total dose per unit time → reduced risk for TD. The dosing interval is every 2–4 weeks. The textbook notes more depot preparations than the slide lists — there are now 8+ available, including paliperidone (Invega Trinza, every 12 weeks) and others.

Slides 49–55 | Drug Therapy Management + Non-Drug Therapy

Management of Schizophrenia — Drug Therapy & Adherence

Slides 49–50 — Drug Selection
📊 Slide Content
  • Three major objectives: (1) suppression of acute episodes, (2) prevention of acute exacerbations, (3) maintenance of highest possible level of functioning
  • Most FGAs and SGAs are equally effective, except for clozapine (more effective than the rest)
  • FGAs: significant risk of EPS | SGAs: risk of metabolic effects | FGAs cost less
📖 Textbook Expansion — Drug Selection Framework

Given that most FGAs and SGAs are equally effective with different side effect profiles, selection should be individualized:

  • Patient with history of diabetes or dyslipidemia: FGA may be preferable (lower metabolic risk); aripiprazole or ziprasidone (SGAs with lowest metabolic risk) are also options
  • Patient who experienced severe EPS on FGA: switch to SGA
  • Treatment-resistant patient (failed ≥2 antipsychotics): trial of clozapine is appropriate
  • No clinical reason to choose SGA over FGA: cost considerations → choose FGA
Slide 51 — Dosing and Routes
📊 Slide Content
  • Dosing is highly individualized
  • Older adult patients require relatively small doses
  • Size and timing of dose likely to change over course of therapy
  • Routes: oral (preferred) — tablets, capsules, liquids; intramuscular; inhaled (loxapine [Adasuve])
📖 Textbook Expansion — Dosing Strategy Over Time

During initial treatment, antipsychotics are given in divided daily doses. Once an effective dosage is established, the entire daily dose can often be given at bedtime — this promotes sleep using the sedative effect while reducing daytime drowsiness.

For long-term maintenance, the goal is the lowest effective dose. Annual attempts should be made to lower the dosage. Older adults need 30–50% less than younger adults. Very large doses are no more effective than moderate doses but increase side effect risk.

Liquid oral formulations are light-sensitive, must be diluted before use, and can cause contact dermatitis (phenothiazines) — staff should avoid skin contact. Sublingual asenapine (Saphris) is absorbed through the oral mucosa and prevents "cheeking."

Slides 52–54 — Treatment Phases and Adherence
📊 Slide Content
  • Initial therapy → Maintenance therapy → Adjunctive drugs (benzodiazepines for anxiety/sleep; antidepressants for depressive symptoms)
  • Promoting adherence: ensure medication is swallowed (check for cheeking); encourage family oversight; provide written and verbal instructions; antipsychotics must be taken on a regular schedule (not PRN)
  • Inform about side effects; assure patients antipsychotics do NOT cause addiction; establish a good therapeutic relationship; use IM depot for long-term therapy
⚠️ Nursing Priority — Non-Adherence is the #1 Cause of Relapse

Poor adherence underlies a significant proportion of hospital readmissions. Barriers to adherence include: anosognosia (lack of insight — patient doesn't believe they're ill), side effects (especially sexual dysfunction and weight gain), cost, stigma, and the feeling of being "cured." Nursing strategies: explore barriers without judgment; address side effects proactively; involve family; consider depot formulations for patients with chronic non-adherence.

Slide 55 — Non-Drug Therapy
📊 Slide Content
  • Counseling for the patient and family
  • Behavioral therapy
  • Vocational training
📖 Textbook Expansion — Comprehensive Non-Drug Program

Medications alone do not constitute optimal treatment. Non-drug components include: (1) Psychotherapy and counseling — helps patient and family understand schizophrenia, facilitates adjustment and rehabilitation. Conventional psychotherapy has limited value in reducing symptoms, but a good therapeutic relationship promotes adherence and facilitates dosage assessment. (2) Behavioral therapy — reduces stress. (3) Vocational training in a sheltered environment — offers the hope of productivity and independence. (4) Social skills training (SST) — improves social performance. Ideally, a comprehensive therapeutic program complements medication. Unfortunately, many patients rely on drugs as their sole treatment.

NCLEX-Style Practice Questions

⭐ High-Yield Topics from These Questions
  • Chlorpromazine contact dermatitis → answer: avoid direct skin contact
  • Tardive dyskinesia presentation → worm-like tongue/face movements (NOT parkinsonism, NOT akathisia, NOT NMS)
  • Clozapine and metabolic syndrome → closely monitor blood glucose in diabetic patient

Q1 (Slide 56): A patient with schizophrenia is prescribed chlorpromazine oral concentrate. Which instruction should the nurse include in the teaching plan?
A. Sexual arousal may be enhanced with this medication   B. Avoid direct skin contact with this medication   C. The medication may cause excessive salivation   D. Do not limit salt intake while taking the medication ▼
✓ Answer: B

Rationale: Handling chlorpromazine (or other antipsychotic oral concentrates) can cause contact dermatitis in patients AND healthcare personnel. Avoid direct contact with these drugs.

  • ✗ A — Sexual dysfunction (not arousal enhancement) is an adverse effect of antipsychotics
  • ✗ C — Dry mouth (not excessive salivation) is the anticholinergic effect of antipsychotics
  • ✗ D — Salt intake restriction is relevant to lithium (bipolar), NOT antipsychotics

Nursing teaching point: Phenothiazines (chlorpromazine class) specifically cause photosensitivity AND contact dermatitis — always use gloves when handling concentrated liquid formulations.

Q2 (Slide 58): Which manifestations does the nurse associate with tardive dyskinesia?
A. Pacing and squirming, uncontrollable need for motion   B. Mask-like face with drooling, tremors, rigidity, shuffling gait   C. Twisting, worm-like movements of the tongue and face   D. Sudden high fever, sweating, and blood pressure fluctuations ▼
✓ Answer: C

Rationale: Tardive dyskinesia = involuntary twisting, worm-like movements of the tongue and face. Late-onset EPS (months to years of therapy).

  • ✗ A — Describes akathisia (motor restlessness; pacing; early EPS)
  • ✗ B — Describes drug-induced parkinsonism (early EPS — weeks)
  • ✗ D — Describes NMS (neuroleptic malignant syndrome — a medical emergency)

Memory trick: TD = Tongue Dancing — worm-like movements late in therapy. Akathisia = can't sit still. Parkinsonism = looks like PD. NMS = fever + "lead pipe" rigidity = emergency.

Q3 (Slide 60): A patient is diagnosed with type 2 diabetes mellitus and schizophrenia. The nurse will closely monitor the blood sugar if the patient receives which medication?
A. Loxapine [Loxitane]   B. Clozapine [Clozaril]   C. Thiothixene [Navane]   D. Haloperidol [Haldol] ▼
✓ Answer: B

Rationale: SGAs carry a higher risk of metabolic effects (diabetes, dyslipidemia) than FGAs. Among SGAs, the risk is greatest with clozapine and olanzapine. Clozapine should be used with caution in patients with diabetes.

  • ✗ A — Loxapine is a medium-potency FGA — lower metabolic risk
  • ✗ C — Thiothixene is a medium-potency FGA — lower metabolic risk
  • ✗ D — Haloperidol is a high-potency FGA — lower metabolic risk

Pattern: All three wrong answers are FGAs. The only SGA is B (clozapine). For metabolic syndrome questions: SGAs > FGAs; and among SGAs, clozapine and olanzapine are highest risk.

Q4 (Slide 62): The nurse prepares to administer the first dose of an antipsychotic agent. One hour after administration, it is MOST important for the nurse to assess what?
A. Range of motion of upper and lower extremities   B. Orthostatic blood pressure measurements   C. Abdomen for distention and bowel sounds   D. Tympanic membrane with an otoscope ▼
✓ Answer: B

Rationale: Antipsychotic drugs block alpha-1 adrenergic receptors → orthostatic hypotension, especially after the first dose. BP and pulse must be checked before dosing and 1 hour afterward, both lying and after sitting/standing for 1–2 minutes.

  • ✗ A — ROM assessment would be for EPS, which typically appears over days, not 1 hour after first dose
  • ✗ C — Bowel sounds/distension are relevant to anticholinergic effects (constipation) but not the priority at 1 hour post-dose
  • ✗ D — No clinical relevance to antipsychotic administration

Hypotension tolerance develops in 2–3 months. The first dose carries the highest risk — always monitor BP post-dose and teach patients to get up slowly from a lying or sitting position.

Exam Priority Review — Module 7
⭐ High-Yield Concepts — Know These Cold
  • FGA MOA: D2 blockade in mesolimbic area = therapeutic; D2 blockade in nigrostriatal area = EPS; D2 blockade in tuberoinfundibular area = ↑ prolactin
  • High-potency FGA (haloperidol): more EPS, less sedation/hypotension/anticholinergic
  • Low-potency FGA (chlorpromazine): less EPS, more sedation/hypotension/anticholinergic
  • TD risk = EQUAL for ALL FGAs regardless of potency
  • EPS sequence: dystonia (hours–5 days) → parkinsonism (5–30 days) → akathisia (5–60 days) → TD (months–years)
  • NMS triad: fever + lead-pipe rigidity + altered LOC → stop drug immediately, ICU, dantrolene, bromocriptine
  • NMS mortality: 5–20%. Temperature can exceed 41°C. More likely with high-potency FGAs.
  • Orthostatic hypotension: check BP lying AND standing 1 hour after first dose; tolerance in 2–3 months
  • Clozapine: most effective antipsychotic; REMS required; agranulocytosis 1–2%; also risk for myocarditis
  • SGAs: less EPS but more metabolic syndrome (weight gain, diabetes, dyslipidemia); clozapine + olanzapine = highest metabolic risk
  • FGAs and SGAs are equally effective overall (clozapine excepted)
  • Depot: every 2–4 weeks; Z-track with 21-gauge needle; relapse rate lower than oral therapy
  • DO NOT use antipsychotics for dementia-related psychosis in older adults (doubles mortality)
